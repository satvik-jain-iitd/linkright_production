LinkRight · journey
what linkright actually is

Not a resume tool. A career navigation OS, with a memory layer at the core.

The current landing page sells "pixel-perfect resumes." That's one atom of a much larger product. LinkRight is the end-to-end job-hunt OS for ambitious folks in India: find roles across portals, rank them against your preferences, reach out with personalised resume + cover letter + LinkedIn DM + recruiter email + portfolio site, prepare for the interview, and build brand visibility with memory-driven content — all powered by a Memory Layer that grows every day you show up.

🧠 the core

The Memory Layer

A structured, retrievable store of you. Built from:

  • Resumes & portfolios you paste
  • Career nuggets from voice interviews
  • Daily diary — what you shipped, learned, struggled with today
  • Interview answers, failed answers, patterns
  • Outcomes — which angles got replies, which didn't

Everything downstream — tailored resume, cover letter, LinkedIn post, interview coach, recruiter DM — retrieves from here. The more you use LinkRight, the sharper it gets.

signal panel · the honest read

The three things. Fix these. Fix these only.

The product does five things. The marketing sells one. The UI exposes 1.5 of them. And the spine — the memory layer — is gated behind a developer tool. Here's the signal.

"If I give you 30 things to fix, you fix zero.
Pick the three that bleed."
🔴 signal 1 · positioning

The landing page sells 20% of the product and hides the other 80%.

LinkRight is a career navigation OS: find · rank · reach out · prepare · broadcast, all powered by a memory layer that grows with daily use. The current landing is "pixel-perfect resumes + 6 feature cards" — and even those cards are all about the resume. The cover letter, LinkedIn DM, recruiter email, portfolio, interview prep, Oracle daily-diary, peer graph, broadcast roadmap — invisible to the prospect.

  • Where: website/src/app/page.tsx + all pricing/marketing copy
  • Fix: rewrite the hero around the loop. Hero = "Find the job. Reach out. Land it. Stay sharp." Under it, 5 pillar cards with one demo each. Make the memory layer the named engine.
  • Impact: a resume tool is a $15/mo commodity. A memory-backed career OS is a $50/mo subscription people stay on for 6+ months.
🔴 signal 2 · the spine is hidden

The memory layer is the product. It's behind Claude Code.

Every other feature — tailored resume, cover letter, recruiter DM, interview prep, broadcast — is just retrieval against the memory layer. If the memory layer is thin, everything degrades. The current onboarding gates it behind "run /interview-coach in Claude Code and upload the JSON." 80%+ of target users can't do that. They skip. Downstream, everything produces worse output. They never see the product at full strength. They churn.

  • Where: src/app/onboarding/StepLifeOS.tsx + missing daily-diary UI on Oracle endpoints
  • Fix: in-product voice interview (60s Q&A, not 15min). Daily diary with a voice button on the home screen. Passive capture: every tailor, every answer, every outcome feeds memory automatically.
  • Impact: depth of memory after 30 days → 10× higher → every downstream artefact is sharper → retention unlocks.
🔴 signal 3 · core pipeline lies & hangs

The one atom that is shipped — the resume — hallucinates filler and hangs at 58%.

Hallucinated filler ("Developed product strategy for enterprise SaaS scale, resulting in improved product development…") ships in the live stream. The landing page brags "0 AI words detected." The build pipeline silently hangs for 6.5+ min — resume_jobs.updated_at never advances, frontend polls happily, no timeout, no error. The one thing users came for is broken and lying.

  • Where: worker/app/pipeline/orchestrator.py phase 5 + worker/app/pipeline/prompts.py
  • Fix: heartbeat + 60s per-phase timeout + user-visible retries. Prompt must refuse to emit a bullet without a source memory atom — fewer bullets > fake bullets.
  • Impact: without this, every other fix is theatre.
✂︎ the cut list

Not redesign. Remove.

  1. The sidebar. Top nav is enough. Two navs = 6 labels for 4 pages + 3 dead links. Kill the sidebar.
  2. The "3 steps" claim on landing. Real flow is 9. Either make it 3, or stop promising 3.
  3. "Custom Apps", "Admin" for non-admins, "Settings" redirect. Dead. Delete.
  4. "Chunks" vs "Highlights" terminology. The user does not care. Pick one word — "atoms" or "highlights".
  5. 4th brand color at 1.0:1 contrast staying in the palette despite failing WCAG.
  6. Auto-fill overwrite of signup email. Silent data mutation. Never.
  7. LinkedIn URL hallucination from name. Leave blank. Ask.
  8. "Skip analysis" button on step 1 of resume builder. Nobody skips the thing they came for.
design crimes · 3 most embarrassing

The ones that show someone stopped caring.

  1. JD analysis · 100% match / 0 gaps. Same Sprinklr bullet matched as "proof" for 6 unrelated JD requirements including "Bachelor's in CS." A trophy for a race the user hasn't run. The product lying to flatter.
  2. Q&A auto-fill pulls the wrong company's story. Question about American Express → answer is the entire Sprinklr + Walmart narrative. Memory retrieval ignores company tags.
  3. Cover letter, LinkedIn DM, recruiter email, portfolio, interview prep — all built in the backend. None exposed in the UI. The team built them, then hid them. Users pay for the 20% they can see.
silent abandonment · where users quietly quit

Not rage-quits. The ones that don't show up in analytics.

  1. Onboarding step 3. User: "am I supposed to install a developer tool? is this for me?" Product: ambiguous copy + a skip that quietly cripples every future feature.
  2. Build phase 5 · 58%. User: "is it still working?" Product: a spinner that hasn't moved in 6 minutes. Tab closed at minute 4.
  3. After the first resume. User: "what else does this do?" Product: a dashboard with 0 matches, 20/20 slots, a dead sidebar. They never learn about cover letters, recruiter DM, interview prep, or daily diary. They don't come back, because nothing pulled them.
overall · noise ratio

Is this team working on the right things?

The team is building fast and a lot. They shipped Scout, Oracle, nuggets, BRS scoring, cover letter, interview prep, portfolio hosting, brand-colour matching, career graph. Then they pointed the landing page at one of those things and hid the rest.

Verdict: the team has built a career OS. They haven't positioned it as one. Until they do, users pay for a broken resume tool while the product quietly contains a $50/mo subscription they never discover.

one more thing

You built the OS. You're selling a calculator.

Every great platform company looked small at launch because they had the discipline to demo one atom, and the ambition to say the big thing out loud. Figma demoed the canvas, but called itself "the collaborative design platform." Linear demoed the issue, but called itself "the tool for high-performance teams." You have a memory-backed career OS with five pillars shipped or half-shipped. You are calling it a resume builder.

The single change with the highest leverage in this whole document is not a bug fix. It is renaming what LinkRight is, on the landing page, in the onboarding, in the dashboard nav. Four verbs — find, reach out, prepare, broadcast — one noun — memory. Everything else follows.
